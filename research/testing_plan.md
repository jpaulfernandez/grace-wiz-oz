**GRACE**

**A Private Companion, Journal, and Documentation Space for Women Navigating Gender-Based Harm**

**_Concept Testing and Validation Plan_**

**Overview**

Grace is a mobile-first private space designed for women in the Philippines to think through, write about, and-if they choose-document experiences of gender-based harm. The product combines three distinct surfaces: a restrained AI Companion that mirrors rather than diagnoses, a journal with free-flow, guided, and incident-log modes, and a controlled export pathway that allows a user to share a synthesized reading of her entries with a clinician, lawyer, or trusted person on her own terms. The design premise is that what survivors most need before they are ready to report is a place to put down what happened in their own words, without surveillance, interpretation, or pressure.

This validation plan addresses a specific challenge: Grace cannot be tested through conventional lean-startup mechanisms such as landing-page conversion tests, fake-door experiments, or behavioral analytics. The product makes claims about emotional safety, restraint of language, and the legal and clinical usefulness of journal-derived artifacts-claims that cannot be validated through proxy metrics. Trust, comfort, and willingness to write inside the app are signals that only surface when real people interact with the product under observed conditions. Likewise, the utility of journal-derived synthesis for case preparation or clinical session work can only be assessed by professionals reading actual outputs.

Accordingly, this plan adopts a qualitative, observation-based validation approach grounded in trauma- and violence-informed research practice, the think-aloud usability protocol, and the Strategyzer Test Card framework for hypothesis tracking. Over a four-week intensive validation phase, the plan tests three core questions: whether women feel safe enough to write inside the app, whether clinicians and lawyers find the journal-derived synthesis useful in their professional practice, and whether those same professionals are willing to onboard as receiving providers on the platform. The validation phase is scoped as a first-pass exercise: its goal is to produce an evidence-based GO, HOLD, or REDESIGN decision for each test track, not a final declaration of problem-solution fit.

**Objectives**

**General Objective**

To validate Grace's user safety, provider utility, and provider onboarding willingness through a structured four-week qualitative validation phase, integrating trauma-informed research practice with standard think-aloud usability methodology, and to produce evidence sufficient to support a disciplined GO, HOLD, or REDESIGN decision for each of three stakeholder tracks.

**Specific Objectives**

- **User Safety and Comfort Validation.** To determine whether women in the target demographic-including survivors of gender-based harm-experience the Grace interface, tone, and Companion behavior as safe enough to write into, by observing live interaction with a clickable prototype paired with a researcher-played Companion, and by measuring self-reported willingness, trust, and safety on calibrated five-point scales.
- **Provider Utility Validation.** To assess whether the synthesis artifacts produced by Grace-the legal lens summary with citation chips and hash-receipted incident entries, and the clinical lens summary with somatic and pattern flags-are useful enough to be incorporated into the actual practice of lawyers handling RA 9262, RA 11313, and related cases, and of clinicians working with women experiencing gender-based harm.
- **Provider Onboarding Validation.** To determine whether clinicians and lawyers are willing to onboard onto Grace as receiving providers-accepting shared links, engaging with the provider-side corpus chat, and integrating the platform into their referral pathway-or whether the platform should instead be scoped as an export-only tool that produces documents providers consume outside the system.
- **Decision-Gate Synthesis.** To consolidate evidence from each track using Learning Cards, document validated and invalidated assumptions, and produce a disciplined GO, HOLD, or REDESIGN recommendation for each track at the end of the four-week phase.

**Methodology and Approach**

This validation phase combines three established research methodologies, each chosen because Grace's subject matter requires a level of methodological care that conventional product testing does not impose. The combined approach is qualitative-led with quantitative anchors: a small number of carefully recruited participants, observed under controlled conditions, with their reactions captured both through structured measurement and through open-ended verbalization.

The first methodological frame is the think-aloud usability protocol, in which participants verbalize their thoughts, reactions, and decisions as they interact with the prototype. Think-aloud is widely considered the most valuable single method for surfacing the why behind user behavior, particularly for products where emotional response and trust formation are central to the experience. It produces direct, in-the-moment data about confusion, hesitation, and felt safety that post-hoc interviews tend to lose.

The second frame is trauma- and violence-informed research practice, drawn from established protocols for conducting research with survivors of gender-based violence. This frame governs every aspect of the survivor cohort sessions: how participants are recruited (through a gatekeeper organization rather than direct outreach), how informed consent is structured (including the right to withdraw at any moment without forfeiting compensation), how the prototype is introduced (without leading framing about the participant's own experience), and how distress is monitored and responded to (with a pre-arranged warm-referral pathway to a partner counselor). This frame is not optional and is treated as a precondition for survivor sessions rather than a phase of the plan.

The third frame is the Strategyzer Test Card and Learning Card, used to translate each test question into a falsifiable hypothesis with explicit success criteria, and to consolidate findings into structured evidence at the end of the phase. Each of the three test tracks below is built around a single Test Card, and findings from each track are recorded on Learning Cards that note what was hypothesized, what was tested, what was learned, and what action the team commits to taking as a result.

A hybrid prototype model supports these methodologies. The clickable prototype, built in Google Stitch, covers the twelve priority screens of the user flow-from the Cold Open to the Three Doors, into the Companion and Journal surfaces, through the post-save soft offers, and into the Crisis Pathway-plus the export flow screens for synthesis preview and provider-side corpus chat. Because a static clickable prototype cannot demonstrate the conversational restraint that is central to the Companion's value, the women's sessions add a Wizard-of-Oz layer: a single researcher plays the Companion live in a chat window, following a written restraint protocol that mirrors the rules the production AI would follow. This allows the validation to test real conversational dynamics-how the participant responds when the AI declines to name her experience, how she reacts to handoff chip offers, whether she trusts the soft suggestion to log an incident-without requiring a working AI backend. The professional cohorts walk through the scripted clickable prototype without the Wizard-of-Oz layer, as their evaluation focuses on the artifacts the system produces rather than on the conversational dynamics.

A consultation with at least one Philippine gender-based violence organization is conducted as a methodological precondition before any survivor session takes place. This consultation serves three purposes: it allows an expert in trauma-informed work with survivors to review the session script for unintended harm; it establishes the referral pathway used in the distress protocol; and it provides the channel through which survivor participants are recruited. The gatekeeper consultation is not itself a validation test, but its outputs-a reviewed protocol and an established referral pathway-are non-skippable inputs to the survivor track.

**Target Audience and Stakeholder Profiles**

The validation phase engages three stakeholder cohorts. Each is profiled below with the jobs, pains, and gains relevant to Grace, followed by the demographic, psychographic, and behavioral characteristics that inform recruitment.

**Women Users**

**Jobs to be Done:** Put down in writing what is happening or has happened, without being told what it is; think through whether to talk to someone about it; preserve a record in case it is needed later; understand the options that exist without committing to any of them.

**Pains:** Lack of a private space to write; fear that what she writes will be seen, judged, or labeled; uncertainty about whether what she experienced "counts"; fear of escalation if she tells anyone; difficulty preserving evidence in a form that would matter later; isolation from others who have experienced similar harm.

**Gains:** A space that does not name her experience for her; a record she controls; the ability to reach a professional when ready, on her own terms; the option to do nothing without losing the record.

**Cohort Composition**

The user cohort consists of eight participants split into two subgroups. Four participants are women who self-identify as having experienced gender-based harm and have considered or attempted to document or report it; this is Grace's primary target user. These participants are recruited exclusively through a partner gender-based violence organization, with all sessions governed by the trauma-informed protocol described in the Ethics and Safety section. The remaining four participants are women in the target demographic-aged twenty to forty, urban or peri-urban, mobile-first, with at least some tertiary education-who do not report active experience of gender-based harm. These participants are recruited through the team's professional network and a brief screener, with sessions following a lighter protocol. The split is intentional: the survivor cohort tests whether the product feels safe for the person it is built for, while the demographic-match cohort tests whether the interface itself reads as safe before someone is in crisis. The two cohorts will be analyzed separately and triangulated, not averaged.

**Clinician Cohort**

**Jobs to be Done:** Build a clinical picture of a patient who may have experienced gender-based harm; prepare for sessions efficiently; receive useful context between sessions without taking on extra unpaid labor; refer patients to safe self-management tools that do not undermine the therapeutic alliance.

**Pains:** Sessions where the patient struggles to articulate what happened; lack of structured patient input between sessions; concern that consumer mental-health apps perform pseudo-therapy and create dependency on the AI; difficulty assessing whether a self-directed tool is trauma-informed enough to recommend.

**Gains:** A structured reading aid that flags somatic patterns, repetition, and naming gaps; a tool the clinician can confidently recommend without worrying about iatrogenic harm; visibility into what the patient is processing between sessions.

**Cohort Composition**

Three to five psychologists or psychiatrists with caseload experience in gender-based harm, intimate partner violence, or sexual violence. Recruitment proceeds through the Psychological Association of the Philippines' practitioner network, partner gender-based violence organizations, and snowball referrals. Participants are compensated for a seventy-five-minute session.

**Lawyer Cohort**

**Jobs to be Done:** Build a case for a client who has experienced gender-based harm; reconstruct a timeline from fragmented client recollection; preserve evidence that will hold up under cross-examination; assess whether digital artifacts produced by a third-party tool are admissible under Philippine Rules on Electronic Evidence.

**Pains:** Clients arrive with incomplete or contradictory recollection; difficulty establishing a contemporaneous record; chain-of-custody questions on consumer-generated digital evidence; the cost of intake hours that could be partially structured.

**Gains:** A synthesis that flags legally-relevant facts with citations to the underlying entries; a hash-receipted, timestamped journal that supports a contemporaneous-record argument; structured intake that reduces case-preparation time.

**Cohort Composition**

Three to five lawyers with active practice in cases involving RA 9262 (Anti-Violence Against Women and Their Children Act), RA 11313 (Safe Spaces Act), or RA 8505 (Rape Victim Assistance and Protection Act). Recruitment proceeds through Saligan, the Integrated Bar of the Philippines' Committee on Women Lawyers and Gender Issues, and partner gender-based violence legal-aid networks. Participants are compensated for a seventy-five-minute session.

**Gatekeeper Consultation**

Although gatekeeper organizations are not a primary validation cohort, at least one Philippine gender-based violence organization-candidates include Lunas Collective, the Women and Gender Institute (WAGI), and partner legal-aid organizations-is consulted before any survivor session is conducted. The gatekeeper reviews the session script for unintended harm, helps identify the referral counselor for the distress protocol, and refers survivor-cohort participants. The gatekeeper's willingness or refusal to engage in this consultation is itself an informal signal: a frontline organization that declines to refer clients to Grace is providing important evidence about the product's positioning in the ecosystem.

**Prototype and Mock-up Description**

The validation phase uses a clickable prototype built in Google Stitch that covers the twelve priority screens of the user flow plus the six-screen export flow. The prototype is sufficient to walk a participant through the full first-contact-to-saved-entry-to-shared-synthesis loop while preserving the visual language and microcopy that the production app will use. Three aspects of the prototype are explicitly not real, and the team commits to acknowledging this with participants before each session begins.

- The AI Companion is played by a single researcher (the wizard) following a written restraint protocol. The prototype includes scripted conversational examples on its Companion screens, but for the women's sessions the wizard takes over after the cold open, allowing real back-and-forth. The wizard is the same person across all eight women's sessions to control for response-pattern drift.
- The hash receipt and timestamp authority shown on the incident-log detail screen are canned visuals. No actual cryptographic anchoring takes place in the prototype. The lawyer cohort is shown the visual and asked to evaluate the concept-the legal argument the design intends to support-rather than its current implementation.
- The provider-side corpus chat shown to the lawyer cohort is a static browser mock with pre-written sample question-and-answer pairs. No live retrieval-augmented generation is performed.

This scope is appropriate for a first-pass validation phase. The team is testing whether the concepts on which Grace's value proposition rests are sound, not whether the engineering required to deliver them has been completed. Participants are made aware of which aspects are demonstrative before being asked to evaluate them. Subsequent validation phases, contemplated in the conclusion of this plan, will test the implemented versions of these features once they have been built.

**Hypothesis Testing Model**

The validation phase consists of three test tracks, one per stakeholder cohort. Each track is governed by a single Strategyzer-style Test Card that translates the test question into a falsifiable hypothesis, specifies the test approach and measured metrics, and defines the success criteria. The three tracks run in parallel during the four-week phase, with the survivor sessions scheduled in the second half of the phase to allow the gatekeeper consultation and the lighter demographic-match sessions to inform any late protocol adjustments.

**Track 1: User Safety and Comfort Validation**

**Test Card 1.1**

**Hypothesis.** We believe that women in the target demographic-including survivors of gender-based harm-will experience Grace's interface, language, and Companion behavior as safe enough to write into, and that this safety perception will hold across the cold open, the journal modes, the handoff between Companion and journal, and the post-save soft offers.

**Test Approach.** Eight sixty-minute moderated sessions with women participants, four with survivors recruited through a gatekeeper organization and four with demographic-match participants from the team's network. Each session walks the participant through the full Tier-1 prototype flow using the concurrent think-aloud protocol. After the Cold Open, the wizard takes over the Companion conversation, allowing real back-and-forth that the static prototype cannot demonstrate. The participant is asked to attempt three core tasks: have a Companion conversation about something currently on her mind, write a journal entry in either Free Flow or Incident Log mode, and review the post-save soft offers. Sessions conclude with a System Usability Scale questionnaire and three custom five-point Likert items on safety, trust, and willingness.

**Metrics.**

- Observed behavior: whether the participant typed anything into the Companion or the journal; how long she sat on the Cold Open before tapping; whether she dismissed the handoff chips; whether she tapped the Crisis button at any point; whether she accepted the AI suggestion to log as incident.
- Think-aloud thematic analysis: surfacing of safety concerns, trust formation moments, points of hesitation, and moments where she explicitly states she would or would not write what she is thinking.
- System Usability Scale (SUS), administered at the end of each session.
- Custom Likert items (1 = strongly disagree, 5 = strongly agree): "I would write what is happening to me in this app," "I trust this app with my words," "I would tell another woman about this app."

**Success Criteria. We are right if:**

- Six or more of eight participants rate "I would write what is happening to me in this app" at 4 or higher.
- No survivor cohort participant flags any specific interaction as actively harmful or unsafe.
- Aggregate SUS score across the cohort is 70 or higher.
- Survivor and demographic-match subgroups do not diverge sharply (no more than a one-point median gap on the safety Likert).
- Think-aloud data surfaces at least three distinct trust-formation moments across the cohort that can be traced to specific design decisions in the prototype.

**Track 2: Clinician Utility and Onboarding Validation**

**Test Card 2.1**

**Hypothesis.** We believe that clinicians working with women experiencing gender-based harm will find the Grace clinical lens synthesis useful enough to incorporate into session preparation, will not flag the Companion's behavior as iatrogenically risky, and will be willing to onboard as receiving providers who accept Grace shared links from patients.

**Test Approach.** Three to five seventy-five-minute moderated sessions with clinicians. Each session begins with a brief overview of the product premise, followed by a guided walkthrough of the full Tier-1 prototype (twelve screens) and the export flow screens relevant to the clinical lens (the synthesis preview screen showing somatic flags and pattern flags, and the provider-side artifact). Particular attention is given to the Companion screens (B1 through B4 and B9), the post-save soft offers (C4 and D4), and the clinical lens synthesis (F5). Participants are asked to evaluate three things: whether the Companion's restraint matches trauma-informed clinical practice, whether the synthesis would be useful for session preparation, and whether they would recommend the platform to a patient. Sessions conclude with the System Usability Scale and three custom Likert items.

**Metrics.**

- Structured rating on each Companion exchange shown: "This exchange is trauma-informed and appropriate" (1-5), captured per exchange.
- System Usability Scale (SUS), administered at the end of each session.
- Custom Likert items: "The clinical lens synthesis would be useful for session preparation," "I would recommend a patient use this between sessions," "I would accept shared links from patients as a receiving provider on this platform."
- Qualitative themes: what is missing from the synthesis, what is unnecessary, where the Companion crosses a line into pseudo-therapy, and what would have to change before the clinician would refer a patient.

**Success Criteria. We are right on utility if:**

- Three or more of five clinicians rate the synthesis usefulness Likert at 4 or higher.
- No clinician flags any specific Companion exchange as risky (rating of 1 or 2 on the trauma-informed scale) without an actionable redesign suggestion.

**We are right on onboarding if:**

- Three or more of five clinicians rate the onboarding Likert at 4 or higher.

These two criteria are evaluated separately. A track that passes on utility but fails on onboarding signals that Grace should be scoped as an export-only tool that produces documents clinicians consume outside the system, rather than as a two-sided platform.

**Track 3: Lawyer Utility and Onboarding Validation**

**Test Card 3.1**

**Hypothesis.** We believe that lawyers handling cases under RA 9262, RA 11313, and RA 8505 will find the Grace legal lens synthesis useful enough to incorporate into case preparation, will not flag the hash receipt and timestamp model as fatally compromised on chain-of-custody grounds, and will be willing to onboard as receiving providers who accept Grace shared links from clients.

**Test Approach.** Three to five seventy-five-minute moderated sessions with lawyers. Each session begins with a brief overview of the product premise and the Philippine Rules on Electronic Evidence framework Grace is designed to support, followed by a guided walkthrough of the full Tier-1 prototype and the export-flow screens relevant to the legal lens (the incident-log detail screen with the hash receipt, the legal lens synthesis preview with citation chips, the share dashboard, and the provider-side corpus chat). Participants are asked to evaluate three things: whether the synthesis would be useful for case preparation, whether the hash-receipt and timestamp model is defensible under cross-examination, and whether they would accept shared links from a client. The team is explicit that the hash receipt shown is a canned visual and that the question is about the concept rather than the current implementation.

**Metrics.**

- System Usability Scale (SUS), administered at the end of each session.
- Custom Likert items: "The legal lens synthesis would be useful for case preparation," "The hash-receipt and timestamp model, if implemented as described, would be defensible under cross-examination," "I would accept shared Grace links from clients as a receiving provider on this platform."
- Qualitative themes: which fields in the incident log are essential for admissibility, what is missing from the synthesis, where the citation chip model breaks down, what would have to change before the lawyer would use this with a client matter.

**Success Criteria. We are right on utility if:**

- Three or more of five lawyers rate the synthesis usefulness Likert at 4 or higher.
- No more than one of five lawyers flags the hash-receipt and timestamp model as fatally flawed; partial reservations (e.g., "this needs an expert witness to introduce") are acceptable and expected.

**We are right on onboarding if:**

- Three or more of five lawyers rate the onboarding Likert at 4 or higher.

As with the clinician track, utility and onboarding are evaluated separately. A utility pass with an onboarding fail signals an export-only positioning rather than a two-sided platform.

**Data Collection Strategy**

Data collection across all three tracks is designed to produce rich qualitative evidence with calibrated quantitative anchors, while honoring the sensitivity of the subject matter and the privacy expectations of every participant. The strategy below applies to all sessions, with track-specific protocols layered on top where required.

**Session Capture**

Audio is recorded for all sessions with explicit informed consent obtained at the start. Screen recording captures the participant's interaction with the prototype. Video of the participant is not recorded by default. Participants are told before recording begins that they can ask for the recording to be paused or stopped at any point, and that pausing or stopping the recording does not affect their compensation. For the survivor cohort, any moment of disclosure that is not directly responsive to the prototype is excluded from the recording on transcription, with a marker indicating that personal disclosure was redacted.

**Transcription and Anonymization**

Recordings are transcribed within forty-eight hours of each session. Transcripts are anonymized at the point of transcription: participant names are replaced with cohort identifiers (e.g., W-S-01 for the first survivor participant, W-D-01 for the first demographic-match participant, C-01 for the first clinician, L-01 for the first lawyer). Original audio recordings are deleted once the anonymized transcript is verified, except where a participant has explicitly consented to their voice being retained for future research. Transcripts and quantitative data are stored locally on encrypted devices and are not synced to any cloud service for the duration of the validation phase.

**Coding and Analysis**

Transcripts are analyzed using a hybrid deductive and inductive thematic coding approach. The deductive codebook is derived from the three test hypotheses and includes anticipated themes such as safety perception, trust formation, hesitation moments, language-restraint reactions, synthesis usefulness, and admissibility concerns. Inductive codes emerge from the data and capture themes the team did not anticipate. The survivor cohort transcripts are independently coded by two team members, with disagreements resolved through discussion before being recorded in the master codebook. Clinician and lawyer transcripts are coded by one team member with a sampled review by a second.

**Quantitative Data**

The System Usability Scale and the custom Likert items are administered at the end of each session on paper or on an anonymized online form. Quantitative results are recorded against the cohort identifier rather than the participant's name. Given the small N within each cohort, results are reported as medians and as counts of participants at each rating level rather than as means, to avoid overstating precision.

**Ethics and Safety Protocol**

Because Grace addresses gender-based harm, the validation phase is conducted under a deliberate ethics and safety protocol that exceeds what would be expected for conventional product testing. This protocol is treated as a precondition for the validation phase rather than as an optional addition, and any session that cannot be conducted in compliance with it is not conducted at all.

**Gatekeeper Consultation**

Before any survivor session takes place, at least one Philippine gender-based violence organization is consulted to review the session script, identify safety language that should be added or modified, and establish the referral pathway used in the distress protocol. The gatekeeper consultation is documented in writing and the consulted organization is given the opportunity to review the final protocol before it is used. The same gatekeeper is also the recruitment channel for the survivor cohort.

**Informed Consent**

Every participant signs an informed consent document at the start of their session. The consent document explains the purpose of the study, what is being recorded, how data will be stored and destroyed, who will see the transcripts, and the participant's right to withdraw at any moment. The consent document explicitly states that withdrawal at any point-including in the middle of a session-does not affect compensation. The consent process is verbal and written; the moderator confirms understanding before recording begins.

**Distress Protocol**

The moderator monitors for signs of distress throughout every session: changes in vocal tone, long pauses, indications that the participant is reflecting on her own experience rather than the prototype, or explicit statements of difficulty. If the moderator observes any of these signs, the session is paused. The moderator offers the participant the option to take a break, to continue with a different part of the prototype, to end the session, or to be referred to the partner counselor identified during the gatekeeper consultation. The distress protocol checklist is included as Appendix D.

**Probing Boundaries**

Moderators are trained to probe reactions to the prototype, not personal disclosures. If a participant volunteers a disclosure-for example, describes an incident from her own life while reacting to the incident-log screen-the moderator acknowledges the disclosure briefly and redirects to the prototype reaction. The moderator does not ask follow-up questions about the disclosure itself. This boundary protects the participant from being used as a research subject for her own experience, and it protects the team from the burden of handling disclosures they are not trained to receive.

**Data Handling**

Original recordings are deleted upon transcript verification. Anonymized transcripts are retained only for the duration of the validation phase analysis (forty-five days after the end of the phase). Quantitative results are retained in aggregate form for inclusion in future product documentation. No raw participant data is shared outside the immediate research team.

**Timeline and Decision Gates**

The validation phase is scoped to four weeks. Because the timeline is tight, the three test tracks run in parallel, with the survivor track scheduled slightly later in the phase to allow the gatekeeper consultation and the lighter demographic-match sessions to inform any late protocol adjustments. A single decision gate at the end of Week 4 evaluates each track separately against its success criteria, producing a GO, HOLD, or REDESIGN recommendation per track.

**Decision Gate Criteria**

**Track 1 (User Safety):**

- GO: Six or more of eight participants at Likert 4+ on willingness; SUS ≥70; no survivor flags an interaction as harmful; survivor and demographic-match subgroups within one median point.
- HOLD: Four or five of eight at Likert 4+; or cohorts diverge sharply (interface reads as friendly to demographic-match but unsafe to survivors). Requires interface iteration and a re-test round with a subset of the survivor cohort.
- REDESIGN: Three or fewer of eight at Likert 4+, or any participant flags an interaction as actively harmful. Triggers a rethink of the affected surface (e.g., Companion restraint, handoff bridge) before any further user testing.

**Track 2 (Clinician):**

- GO: Utility and onboarding Likerts each pass (≥3 of 5 at 4+). Proceed to clinician-side MVP development.
- HOLD on onboarding only: Utility passes but onboarding does not. Reframe Grace as an export-only product on the clinical side; clinicians consume the synthesis as a PDF rather than logging in. This is a viable Phase 2 positioning.
- REDESIGN: Any clinician flags a Companion exchange as risky without an actionable redesign suggestion, or fewer than three of five pass utility. Pause clinician-facing development and revisit Companion restraint protocol.

**Track 3 (Lawyer):**

- GO: Utility and onboarding Likerts each pass; no more than one lawyer flags the hash model as fatally flawed.
- HOLD on admissibility: Utility passes but two or more lawyers flag the hash model as fatally flawed without it. Triggers a focused legal-expert deep-dive on the Rules on Electronic Evidence and a possible redesign of the timestamp anchoring approach before lawyer-side onboarding development proceeds.
- REDESIGN: Fewer than three of five pass utility, or three or more flag the synthesis as not introducible at trial. Reconsider whether legal-side positioning is correct, or pivot to clinician-side first.

| Researcher confirmation bias                | Two-coder thematic analysis on the survivor cohort with documented disagreement resolution. Quantitative Likert items administered on paper or anonymized form to reduce social-desirability bias toward the moderator. Negative findings explicitly invited at the end of each session ("What would make this worse for someone you know?").                                                                                                           |

**Conclusion: From Validation to Action**

At the end of Week 4, the team applies the decision-gate criteria to each of the three tracks and produces a recommendation memo. The recommendation is track-specific: a GO on one track does not require a GO on the others, and the combinations of outcomes produce different next steps for product development.

**If All Three Tracks Achieve GO**

Proceed to a sixteen-week MVP build covering the validated screens and the export flow. Confirm the gatekeeper partnership for ongoing user research access. Recruit a closed beta of fifteen to twenty women users (through the gatekeeper), three to five clinicians, and three to five lawyers. Schedule the second-pass validation phase with the implemented (rather than wizard-played) Companion and the actual cryptographic timestamp anchoring.

**If User Track Passes and One Professional Track Fails**

Proceed to MVP build on the user side and on the professional side that passed. Reframe the failed professional side as a Phase 2 deferred validation; if the failure was on onboarding only, reposition the failed side as an export-consumption side (the professional receives a PDF rather than logging in). This is a structurally workable outcome and does not require pivoting the underlying product.

**If User Track Fails on Safety**

Pause all professional-side development. Investigate the specific safety failure: was it the Companion's restraint protocol, the handoff bridge between Companion and journal, the post-save soft offers, or the Crisis Pathway? Redesign the implicated surface and re-test with a subset of the survivor cohort before resuming any MVP build. This is the one failure mode that gates everything else, because Grace cannot serve any provider relationship if users will not write into it.

**If Two or More Tracks Fail**

Reconsider whether the Grace concept-a single product that serves women, clinicians, and lawyers-is the right unit of validation. Possible pivots include narrowing to a single stakeholder (e.g., a journal product for women only, with no provider side), partnering with an existing GBV organization rather than building a standalone consumer product, or reframing the value proposition entirely. The Learning Cards from this phase remain useful inputs to whichever next step is chosen.

**Appendices**

**Appendix A - Strategyzer Test Card Template**

The Strategyzer Test Card format is used for each of the three test tracks above (Test Cards 1.1, 2.1, and 3.1) and consists of four fields: Hypothesis (We believe that...), Test (To verify that, we will...), Metric (And measure...), and Criteria (We are right if...). Blank templates are kept in the team's working folder for any additional tests that emerge during the validation phase.

**Appendix B - Session Script Outlines**

Three session scripts are maintained, one per cohort. Each script consists of a script header (session purpose, materials needed, recording setup), an informed-consent block, a warm-up section, a screen-by-screen walkthrough with prompts (think-aloud cues, attention-direction prompts, role-specific questions), a post-walkthrough debrief, the SUS questionnaire, the custom Likert items, and a closing block. The women's session script differs from the clinician and lawyer scripts in two material ways: (1) the warm-up section is shorter and lower-stakes, and (2) the distress protocol activation cues are listed in the moderator's margin notes.

**Appendix C - Custom Likert Items**

All custom Likert items use a five-point scale (1 = strongly disagree, 5 = strongly agree). Women's cohort: "I would write what is happening to me in this app," "I trust this app with my words," "I would tell another woman about this app." Clinician cohort: "The clinical lens synthesis would be useful for session preparation," "I would recommend a patient use this between sessions," "I would accept shared links from patients as a receiving provider on this platform." Lawyer cohort: "The legal lens synthesis would be useful for case preparation," "The hash-receipt and timestamp model, if implemented as described, would be defensible under cross-examination," "I would accept shared Grace links from clients as a receiving provider on this platform."

**Appendix D - Distress Protocol Checklist**

Pre-session: confirm referral counselor availability for the day of the session; verify that the consent document includes the right-to-withdraw clause; place the contact card for the referral counselor within reach of the moderator. During the session, the moderator monitors for distress signs: vocal-tone shift, long pauses (more than ten seconds without interaction with the prototype), self-referential language indicating the participant is reflecting on her own experience rather than reacting to the prototype, or explicit statements of difficulty. If any sign is observed, the moderator pauses the session, asks a single open question ("Would you like to take a break, continue with a different part, or stop here?"), and respects the participant's choice without probing. If the participant chooses to stop or accepts the referral, the moderator confirms next-of-contact preferences (the participant initiates the referral, not the team) and ends the session with full compensation.
